# Delivery Guide: Can a Container Be Delivered to My Property?

**Decision:** D3 - Delivery Feasibility | **Word count:** ~1,400 | **Target queries:** "container delivery requirements", "shipping container delivery"

---

## Quick Answer

Most rural properties can receive container delivery without issues. You need:

- **Width:** At least 10 feet of clearance for the truck
- **Height:** At least 14 feet of overhead clearance
- **Length:** Room for a ~65-foot truck to position (or tilt-bed if tight)
- **Ground:** Reasonably firm, level surface for placement

If your driveway fits a large delivery truck or farm equipment transporter, it can probably fit a container delivery.

---

## How Containers Are Delivered

Understanding the delivery method helps you assess whether your property works.

### Tilt-Bed Truck (Most Common)

The container sits on a flatbed trailer. At your site, the truck tilts the bed and slides the container off onto the ground.

**Requirements:**
- Truck length: ~60-65 feet total (truck + trailer + container)
- The truck needs to position with the back of the trailer where you want the container
- Ground should be firm enough that the container doesn't sink as it slides off
- Works for both 20-foot and 40-foot containers

**Best for:** Properties with good driveway access and space to position

### Crane Delivery

A separate crane lifts the container off a flatbed and places it. The truck doesn't need to position exactly at the final spot.

**Requirements:**
- Crane needs access close enough to reach the placement site
- More expensive than tilt-bed
- Can place containers in tighter spots

**Best for:** Locations where the truck can't reach the exact placement spot

### Chassis Drop

The container arrives on a wheeled chassis (the frame semi-trucks use). The chassis is disconnected and left with the container on wheels.

**Requirements:**
- Container stays on wheels (elevated)
- May need to be moved later to permanent ground placement
- Less common for permanent storage

## Access Requirements

### Driveway Width

**Minimum:** 10 feet wide
**Comfortable:** 12+ feet wide

The delivery truck is about 8.5 feet wide. You need clearance on both sides for mirrors and maneuvering.

**Check for:**
- Narrow gates or fence openings
- Trees or posts that pinch the driveway
- Mailboxes, signs, or other obstacles

### Overhead Clearance

**Minimum:** 14 feet

A container on a truck sits about 13-14 feet high. Check for:
- Low-hanging branches
- Power lines (call your utility if concerned)
- Covered bridges or structures

### Turning Radius

A 65-foot truck combination needs room to turn. Consider:
- 90-degree turns off a public road
- Tight curves in your driveway
- Dead ends where the truck would need to back out

**Rural driveways usually work.** Long straight driveways with gentle curves are ideal. Sharp switchbacks are challenging.

### Ground Conditions

**For tilt-bed delivery:**
- Ground should be firm enough to support the truck
- Soft, muddy, or sandy areas may not work
- Gravel or compacted earth is fine
- Concrete or asphalt is ideal

**For final placement:**
- Container should sit on level ground
- Some sellers provide railroad ties or blocks to level and elevate
- Sitting directly on dirt is okay but may cause faster bottom rust

### Tip: Walk the Route

Walk from the road to where you want the container. Note anything that might be tight:
- Trees close to the edge
- Ditches or drop-offs
- Soft spots that might not hold a heavy truck
- Overhead wires or branches

## Placement Considerations

### Where Should the Container Go?

Think about:
- **Access:** Can you easily get to it with vehicles, equipment?
- **Drainage:** Will water pool around it?
- **Sun/shade:** Full sun keeps it dry; shade may increase condensation
- **Visibility:** Does placement matter for aesthetics?
- **Future use:** If you might add a second container, leave room

### Ground Preparation

**Minimum:** Clear, relatively level ground
**Better:** Gravel pad or compacted base
**Best:** Concrete pad or railroad tie foundation

Most agricultural buyers place containers directly on firm ground or a gravel pad. This works fine for storage.

### Door Orientation

Containers have doors on one end. Make sure:
- Doors face the direction you want
- There's room to swing doors fully open (about 9 feet of clearance)
- You can back a truck or trailer to the door if needed

## Common Delivery Questions

### What if my driveway is too tight?

Options:
- Crane delivery (container placed by crane rather than slid off)
- Temporary widening (remove obstacles, cut back branches)
- Alternative placement closer to road access
- Smaller container (20-foot is easier to deliver than 40-foot)

### What if I have a long driveway?

Long driveways are usually fine if they're reasonably straight. The truck just needs to reach your placement site. Distance isn't the issue—turns and obstacles are.

### Can delivery happen in winter?

Depends on conditions. Frozen ground is actually firm and works well. Mud or snow-covered driveways may delay delivery until conditions improve.

### Who decides if delivery is possible?

The seller will discuss your site conditions before confirming delivery. Photos or a description of your access help them determine the right approach.

### What if I'm not sure?

Describe your property when requesting a quote. Include:
- Driveway length and surface type
- Any gates, turns, or overhead obstacles
- Where you want the container placed

The seller can advise whether standard delivery works or if alternatives are needed.
