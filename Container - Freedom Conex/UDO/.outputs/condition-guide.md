# Condition Guide: New vs. Used Shipping Containers

## Quick Answer

| Condition | What You Get | Best For |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| **New (One-Trip)** | Like-new, minimal wear, no rust | Conversions, visible placement, long-term use |
| **Used - Cargo Worthy** | Structurally sound, cosmetic wear, some dents/rust | General storage, farm use, budget-conscious |
| **Used - Wind & Water Tight** | Keeps weather out, more wear, retired from shipping | Basic storage where appearance doesn't matter |
| **As-Is** | Significant wear, may need repair | Parts, projects, very tight budgets |

**The short version:** Used containers cost 40-60% less than new. For most storage purposes, cargo-worthy used containers work fine.

## Understanding Container Grades

Shipping containers are graded by their condition and remaining useful life. These grades are industry-standard, though exact naming varies by seller.

### New / One-Trip

**What it means:** The container was manufactured overseas, loaded with cargo for one voyage, and sold upon arrival. It's effectively new.

**What you'll see:**
- Original paint, minimal fading
- No dents or patches
- No rust (or only surface flash rust that wipes off)
- Clean interior floors
- Doors open and close smoothly
- Factory seals intact

**Best for:**
- Conversions to offices, workshops, or living spaces
- Placement where appearance matters (visible from road, near house)
- Storing items sensitive to dust or contamination
- Maximum lifespan (20+ years with minimal maintenance)

**Trade-off:** Costs significantly more than used.

### Cargo Worthy (CW)

**What it means:** The container is structurally sound and certified for international shipping. It has wear from years of service but remains fully functional.

**What you'll see:**
- Faded paint, surface rust spots
- Minor dents (don't affect structure)
- Possible small patches or repairs (welded, sealed)
- Floor may show wear but is solid
- Doors seal properly
- Weatherproof

**Best for:**
- General equipment and material storage
- Farm and agricultural use
- Situations where function matters more than appearance
- Budget-conscious buyers who want reliability

**Trade-off:** Cosmetic imperfections. You're buying a working tool, not a showpiece.

### Wind & Water Tight (WWT)

**What it means:** The container is retired from shipping but still keeps weather out. It may have failed cargo-worthy inspection due to cosmetic issues, not structural problems.

**What you'll see:**
- More visible wear than CW
- More dents, more surface rust
- May have larger patches
- Floor wear more pronounced
- Still seals against wind and rain

**Best for:**
- Storage where weatherproofing is the only requirement
- Back-of-property placement
- Items that aren't moisture-sensitive
- Tightest budgets for functional storage

**Trade-off:** Shorter remaining lifespan, more maintenance over time.

### As-Is / Sold As-Is

**What it means:** The container is sold without guarantees. It may have holes, failed seals, floor damage, or other issues.

**What you'll see:**
- Varies widely—inspect carefully
- May need welding or repair
- May not be weatherproof without work

**Best for:**
- Buyers who can repair or don't need weatherproofing
- Parts containers
- Very low-budget projects

**Trade-off:** You may spend more fixing it than you saved buying it.

## What to Inspect (Any Condition)

Whether buying new or used, check these before accepting delivery:

### Exterior

- **Roof:** Look for dents, pooling areas, rust-through
- **Walls:** Check for holes, deep dents, patch quality
- **Doors:** Open and close both doors fully; check seals
- **Corner castings:** Ensure they're not cracked or bent (structural)
- **Forklift pockets:** Check for damage if you'll move it later

### Interior

- **Floor:** Walk the entire length; check for soft spots, holes
- **Walls:** Look for light coming through (indicates holes)
- **Ceiling:** Check for rust, condensation damage
- **Smell:** Musty smell may indicate past water intrusion

### Doors and Seals

- **Gaskets:** Rubber seals should be pliable, not cracked
- **Locking bars:** Should move freely, engage properly
- **Hinges:** Check for excessive rust or binding

## Which Condition Should You Choose?

### Choose New/One-Trip If:

- You're converting to workspace, office, or living space
- The container will be visible (driveway, near buildings)
- You're storing moisture-sensitive items
- You want minimal maintenance for 20+ years
- Appearance matters to you

### Choose Cargo Worthy If:

- You need reliable storage without paying for looks
- It's going in a back field or non-visible location
- You're storing equipment, materials, or supplies
- You want the best balance of cost and condition
- You're a practical buyer focused on function

### Choose Wind & Water Tight If:

- Budget is your primary constraint
- You only need basic weatherproof storage
- Appearance truly doesn't matter
- Contents aren't sensitive to minor imperfections
- You accept shorter lifespan and potential maintenance

### Choose As-Is If:

- You can inspect in person before buying
- You have welding/repair capabilities
- The price difference is substantial
- You're willing to do work to make it functional

## Common Questions

### Do used containers have odors?

Sometimes. Containers that carried certain cargo may have residual smells. Cargo-worthy containers from reputable sellers are typically aired out and cleaned. If you're concerned, ask about the container's shipping history or request one that carried dry goods.

### Can I paint a used container?

Yes. Many buyers paint used containers to improve appearance and add rust protection. Proper prep (cleaning, rust treatment, primer) is important for paint to adhere.

### How long will a used container last?

Cargo-worthy containers typically have 15+ years of useful life for storage. Wind & water tight may have 10+ years. Actual lifespan depends on your climate, placement (on ground vs. elevated), and maintenance.

### Is the floor safe to walk on?

Container floors are marine-grade plywood, typically treated for pest resistance. Standard floors are safe for storage use. If you're converting to workspace where people will spend significant time, some buyers replace or cover the floor. For agricultural storage, standard floors are fine.

### Are there different sizes of each grade?

Yes. New and used containers are both available in 20-foot, 40-foot, and 40-foot high cube sizes. See our [Size Guide →] for dimensions.
