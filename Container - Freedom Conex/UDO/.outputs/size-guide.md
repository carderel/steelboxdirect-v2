## Quick Answer

Most buyers choose between two sizes:

| Size | Exterior | Interior Floor Space | Best For |
|------|----------|---------------------|----------|
| **20-foot** | 20' x 8' x 8'6" | ~160 sq ft | Single vehicles, seasonal equipment, moderate storage |
| **40-foot** | 40' x 8' x 8'6" | ~320 sq ft | Large equipment, workshops, bulk storage |
| **40-foot High Cube** | 40' x 8' x 9'6" | ~320 sq ft (taller) | Same as 40', plus overhead clearance needs |

## The Three Standard Sizes

Shipping containers were built for international cargo, so dimensions are standardized worldwide. This means a "20-foot container" is the same size whether it comes from our yard or anywhere else.

### 20-Foot Container

**Exterior dimensions:** 20' long x 8' wide x 8'6" tall
**Interior dimensions:** 19'4" long x 7'8" wide x 7'10" tall
**Floor space:** ~160 square feet
**Volume:** ~1,170 cubic feet

**Works well for:**
- One full-size tractor or ATV
- 8-10 pallets of materials
- Seasonal equipment (tillers, mowers, attachments)
- Feed storage for small operations
- Workshop with limited tool storage

**Consider if:**
- Your property has tight access or limited space
- You're storing a defined set of items (not accumulating over time)
- Budget is a primary concern

### 40-Foot Container

**Exterior dimensions:** 40' long x 8' wide x 8'6" tall
**Interior dimensions:** 39'5" long x 7'8" wide x 7'10" tall
**Floor space:** ~320 square feet
**Volume:** ~2,390 cubic feet

**Works well for:**
- Multiple vehicles or large equipment
- 20+ pallets of materials
- Workshop with dedicated work area plus storage
- Hay storage for medium operations
- Equipment that needs to be stored assembled

**Consider if:**
- You expect storage needs to grow
- You want workspace inside, not just storage
- You're storing equipment that's awkward to disassemble

### 40-Foot High Cube

**Exterior dimensions:** 40' long x 8' wide x 9'6" tall
**Interior dimensions:** 39'5" long x 7'8" wide x 8'10" tall
**Floor space:** ~320 square feet (same as standard 40')
**Volume:** ~2,700 cubic feet

**The only difference from a standard 40-foot is the extra 12 inches of interior height.**

**Works well for:**
- Tall equipment (combines, sprayers with booms)
- Workshops where you'll be standing (more comfortable headroom)
- Stacking pallets three-high
- Converting to office or workspace

**Consider if:**
- Standard height feels cramped for your use
- You need vertical storage capacity
- Delivery access allows the extra height (check clearances)

## Factors That Affect Your Choice

### 1. What You're Storing Now vs. Later

If you have a defined list of items, size accordingly. If you expect to accumulate more over time, go larger. Upgrading later means buying a second container or replacing the first—neither is cheap.

### 2. How You'll Access Contents

A 40-foot container stores twice as much, but items at the back are 40 feet from the door. Consider:
- Will you need to access everything regularly?
- Can items be organized so frequently-used things stay near the front?
- Would two 20-foot containers work better than one 40-foot?

### 3. Property Space and Placement

Containers need flat, level ground. Measure your intended location:
- Is there room for the full length plus door swing?
- Can a delivery truck access the spot?
- Will the container block anything important?

### 4. Delivery Truck Access

The truck that delivers your container needs space to maneuver. Generally:
- 20-foot containers are easier to place in tight spots
- 40-foot containers need more room for the truck to position

See our [Delivery Guide →] for specific requirements.

### 5. Future Flexibility

If you might convert the container to a workshop, office, or other use later:
- 40-foot gives more usable space
- High Cube gives more comfortable headroom for standing work

## The "Two 20s vs. One 40" Question

Sometimes buyers wonder if two 20-foot containers are better than one 40-foot.

**Two 20-foot containers might be better if:**
- You want to separate categories of items (tools vs. materials)
- Placement requires flexibility (different locations on property)
- Access is easier to two smaller spaces
- You want to buy one now and add another later

**One 40-foot container is usually better if:**
- Total cost matters (one 40 costs less than two 20s)
- You want connected workspace
- Delivery is a one-time event you'd rather not repeat
- Large equipment needs continuous floor space
